# IWD 2026 — Portfolio Case Study

---

## Overview

An editorial-style single-page web experience built for Lovable's International Women's Day 2026 community event. The piece investigates the gender gap across design, AI, and engineering. Research-driven, politically intentional, aesthetically rigorous.

**Role:** Solo — concept, research, design direction, build
**Platform:** Lovable (first time using)
**Status:** Complete and live

---

## Problem

The gender gap in design, AI, and engineering is well-documented but under-visualized. Most IWD content is either data-free inspiration or data-heavy without emotional weight. Neither lands. I wanted to make something that felt urgent — because it is.

Working in a male-dominated environment made this personal, not abstract.

---

## Design Direction

- **Suffragette palette** — deep purple, forest green, white. Yellow as graphic accent only.
- **Protest print aesthetics** — halftone screens, duotone photo treatment, grain overlay, hard offset shadows, headline misregistration
- **Typography** — Bebas Neue / Druk Wide for display; IBM Plex Mono for body/data; Courier Prime for accent

Explicitly rejected: pink, decorative serifs, gradients, rounded corners, gentle shadows, anything that reads as soft or trend-chasing.

---

## AI-Assisted Workflow

1. **Research + synthesis in Claude** — grounded in sources, not vibes
2. **Iterative brief development in Claude** — multiple rounds of refinement before touching a build tool
3. **Build execution in Lovable** — with a resolved brief, not a vague idea

---

## Outcome

- Built and published within the event timeframe
- Canadian Design Network article published
- LinkedIn post drafted and published
- Demonstrated the full Claude → Lovable pipeline as a repeatable creative process

---

## What It Proved

- Soft aesthetics don't serve hard-edged content — visual language and message must be aligned
- Research-first workflow produces more defensible design decisions
- Lovable can execute resolved briefs quickly and well
- Personal experience in a male-dominated workplace is a legitimate research lens, not a disclaimer
